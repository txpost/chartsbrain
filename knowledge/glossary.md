# Chart-pattern glossary — patterns, signals, frameworks

**This is your seed glossary — yours to grow.** It's the methodology layer for ChartsBrain: the *meaning* of the pattern tags you classify charts with. **chartsdb itself is a dumb corpus** (screenshots + thin descriptive metadata + free tags); the meaning of those tags — what a VCP is, what an Episodic Pivot is, how a Wedge Pop differs from a wedge — lives *here*, in the glossary the `chart-ingest` skill reads when classifying a new chart.

Treat it as a **starting point, not an authority.** Use these definitions as-is, edit them to match how *you* see setups, add new patterns as you learn them, or strip it down and build your own from scratch. The skill classifies against whatever's in this file — when it meets a chart that fits no pattern here, it flags it for you to add. Your glossary is your curriculum; grow it in whatever direction you trade.

The definitions below are terse (1–3 sentences each), with attributions to the traders they come from (O'Neil, Minervini, Qullamaggie, Bonde, Zanger, TraderLion, and others). Append new patterns/signals/frameworks under the appropriate section as your collection grows.

## Patterns


A pattern is a multi-bar formation. In the corpus these appear as free tags (e.g. `high-tight-flag`).

- **buying-in-bases** — Nick Schmidt's setup: entering *during* a controlled pullback/base **inside an already-established Stage-2 uptrend** (the continuation partner to [[#character-change]], which catches the trend's *birth*). "Breakouts do not come from nowhere. They come from bases." The base must come up its right side **tighter and lighter**: **higher lows + tightening candles + a volume dry-up** ("tight price action usually means someone with real size has a plan"). **Entry on weakness, not strength** — near a higher low, a weekly low, or a base-pivot retest, *not* at the top of the range — with the stop just under the most recent higher low (Schmidt's target risk **3–5%**), which is the whole edge over chasing the breakout (tighter stop, bigger size, quieter timing, cushion already built in). **A high-conviction overlay — `ma-compression`:** the 10W and 30W converge tightly (~within 5%) while price coils and volume dries up, meaning short- *and* intermediate-term holders share one cost basis; when MA-compression + tightening + dry-up coincide the setup is unusually strong (first seen on `2026-05-31-ONDS-W-buying-in-bases`). The **first base after a [[#character-change]]** is the highest-conviction version (no pyramiding risk — you're at the trend's start); in a sustained Stage 2 the pattern *repeats*, so catch the *next* base if you missed the first (each resets its own stop). Full method + pre-trade checklist: Nick Schmidt's “Buying in Bases” writeup (nickschmidt.so). (Schmidt framework; maps to Weinstein Stage-2 continuation.)
- **buying-in-bases-imperfect** — buying-in-bases setup where some criteria fail but conviction overlay justifies entry.
- **character-change** — a stage transition: a stock breaking from stage-1 (basing) into stage-2 (advancing) — the same event as a Weinstein [[#stage-analysis|Stage 1→2]] transition, read on the **weekly** ("weekly charts cut through the noise and show the real trend"). Nick Schmidt's framework requires **three pieces of evidence to align** (first two in either order; third optional but powerful):
  1. **Volume surge** — the biggest volume in months *paired with a strong price move up* = institutional accumulation ("big funds can't buy hundreds of thousands of shares quietly"). **Read where the candle closes, not just the bar height:** a huge spike on a *down* week or a reversal closing weak is *liquidation*, not accumulation — the same-size bar, opposite meaning. This is Schmidt's false-signal filter.
  2. **Higher low** — after a prolonged downtrend, price makes a higher low on the weekly (seller exhaustion + buyers entering at elevated prices). A higher low *alone* lacks institutional conviction — it needs the volume.
  3. **Moving-average respect, with the "relevant looking left" filter** — price pulls back to and holds a key MA (10W/30W/4W) for the *first time* since the downtrend began. **The MA must have been acting as resistance during the decline** ("the moving average that had been holding it down was now holding it up"). An MA the stock chopped through repeatedly is *not* relevant; the reclaim of one that *capped every rally on the way down* is the high-value tell. This filter is the single most-skipped, most-discriminating check.
  - **Entry:** once 1+2 align you have a likely new trend; with all three, buy the pullback to the MA with a tight stop just below it (multiple entry attempts allowed as the trend develops).
  - **W-shape advance notice:** character changes often emerge from a **higher-low double-bottom** — the interim peak becomes the neckline, and breaking it makes the volume-surge + higher-low + MA-reclaim align *by construction*. Once the second (higher) low is in and climbing on declining volume with the neckline visible, you can **pre-position the watchlist weeks before** the criteria fire — anticipating the character change instead of reacting to it. (See Nick Schmidt's writeups on the higher-low double-bottom → character-change → Stage-2-base sequence.)
  - **Group confirmation:** a character change confirms harder when the cohort is also setting up / rotating (a lone clean chart is weaker). The sequential partner is [[#buying-in-bases]] (the *first base* after a character change is the highest-conviction buying-in-bases entry). Full method + checklists: Nick Schmidt's “Character Change” writeup (nickschmidt.so). Corpus examples: `2009-03-15-CMG-W-stage-1-to-2-trevor`, `2025-05-15-TAN-W-stage-1-to-2-trevor` (MA-reclaim form), `2024-11-15-TWLO-W-stage-1-to-2-breakout-trevor` (base-breakout + retest form). (Schmidt framework; maps to Weinstein Stage 1→2.)
- **new-momentum-zone** — first sustained rise above a rising 10wk MA and flattening-to-rising 40wk MA after a Stage 4 decline. The zone within which entry setups become valid. (Moglen / Schmidt-adjacent.)
- **flat-base** — multi-week sideways consolidation, typically <15% deep, that resolves with a breakout. As a *pattern* (vs. a base shape), the flat base IS the setup. **Dan Zanger** ([chartpattern.com](https://www.chartpattern.com/flat-base.cfm)): a stock that goes horizontal for any length of time with volume drying up; draw a trendline across the top and buy the break of that line on increasing volume — very powerful advances come from it. A flat base that holds firm through a broad market sell-off is a "**base on a base**," a positive relative-strength tell. Corpus example: `1999-02-28-BVSN-D-flat-base-zanger`.
- **channel** — *continuation*. Price vacillates between **two parallel trendlines** (a rectangle when horizontal, or a sloped channel) — supply and demand evenly matched, repeatedly testing the same highs and lows. Volume lessens within the pattern and noticeably increases on the breakout; channels usually resolve **in the direction of the prior trend**. (Dan Zanger, [chartpattern.com](https://www.chartpattern.com/channel-formation.cfm).) Close kin to `range-consolidation` / `flat-base` — the distinction Zanger draws is the explicit *parallel-line* framing (buy near the lower line, sell near the upper, trade the breakout). A horizontal channel and a flat base can be the same structure read two ways: corpus example `1999-02-28-BVSN-D-flat-base-zanger` is tagged both (Zanger reused the one BVSN chart to teach both patterns).
- **flag-breakout** — a continuation pattern where price consolidates in a tight downward-sloping range after an advance, resolved by a breakout in the direction of the prior trend.
- **range-breakout** — a tight horizontal range that resolves with a breakout in either direction. Continuation pattern.
- **vcp** — Volatility Contraction Pattern. Successive contractions of price range and volume forming a tightening structure. (Minervini framework.)
- **multi-phase-trend** — case-study label for entries that span multiple regimes/phases (Uptrend 1 → Key Base → Uptrend 2 → topping). The entry covers the whole arc, not a single setup.
- **model-book-move** — case-study label for a textbook market-leader move (3-figure % move, multiple bases, clean regime transitions). A multi-phase-trend that earned model-book status.
- **cup-with-handle** — O'Neil-style base shape AS a pattern: extended cup-shaped consolidation with a brief downward-drifting handle resolving with a breakout.
- **episodic-pivot** — Pradeep Bonde's term. A high-volume gap-up day that fundamentally re-rates the stock, used as an entry trigger. The episode itself is the setup; the chart shows the gap.
- **high-tight-flag** — O'Neil pattern: a stock that has rallied 100%+ in 4-8 weeks then consolidates tightly for 3-5 weeks before breaking out again. Rare and powerful.
- **oops-reversal** — Larry Williams' term for a gap-down open that reverses to close above the prior day's low. The "oops" is the gap-down that gets bought back. Entry on the reversal.
- **parabolic-curve** — Dan Zanger's term ([chartpattern.com](https://www.chartpattern.com/parabolic-curve.cfm)). The end result of multiple base-formation breaks stacking into an accelerating, ever-steepening advance — price curves upward as each leg gets sharper, drawn with a series of **rising trendlines of increasing steepness**. Found at or near the **end of a major market advance**; yields the biggest, quickest gains but ends in a blowoff top. The move tops when the steepest line breaks (on high volume); successive breaks of the lower rising lines mark further failure/short points. Distinct from Qullamaggie's [[#parabolic-short]] (a *trade* with VWAP entry mechanics) and Kell's [[#exhaustion-extension]] (an MA-extension framing) — the parabolic-curve names the multi-base accelerating *formation* itself. Corpus example: `2000-01-04-BVSN-D-parabolic-curve-zanger` (Zanger's blowoff-top example, the same BVSN whose `1999-02-28` flat base launched the run).
- **climax-top** (blowoff top) — the terminal event of a [[#parabolic-curve|parabolic]] advance: price has gone vertical, accelerating into a euphoric peak, then **reverses violently on the heaviest volume of the entire move** — supply overwhelming demand at the moment sentiment is most bullish. The bearish mirror of an accumulation bottom (the crowd that chased it up becomes the supply). Composed of components the glossary names separately — a [[#parabolic-curve]] advance, a [[#key-reversal-bar]] (strong-volume reversal after a mature trend), Kell's [[#exhaustion-extension]], and a [[#volume-surge]] of distribution — `climax-top` is the single tag for the *event* those converge into. Often coincides with a great fundamental catalyst being **sold** (the news is the occasion, not the cause), and frequently begins a [[#stage-analysis|Stage-4]] markdown. Minervini's framing: "stories, earnings reports, and valuation do not move stock prices; people do." Corpus example: `2007-11-01-CROX-W-climax-top-minervini` (Crocs reported earnings +144% and fell 36% that day on record volume, then collapsed ~$75 → ~$1). (Minervini, *Think and Trade Like a Champion*.)
- **wedge** — classic Edwards-&-Magee converging-trendline formation: two trendlines coming together at an apex with a noticeable slant. Volume diminishes into the apex and increases on the resolve. The slant names the variant. **falling-wedge** (lower tops + lower bottoms) is generally *bullish*; **rising-wedge** (higher tops + higher bottoms) is generally *bearish*. (Dan Zanger, [chartpattern.com](https://www.chartpattern.com/wedge-formation.cfm).) **Distinct from Kell's [[#wedge-pop]] / [[#wedge-drop]]** — those are MA-based breakout/breakdown signals in the Cycle of Price Action, not this converging-trendline apex structure. *Caveat:* the bullish/bearish defaults are tendencies, not laws — Zanger's own corpus example `1999-02-12-CNET-D-rising-wedge-zanger` is a *rising* wedge he **buys** on an upside breakout, resolving opposite his stated rising=bearish rule (a low-float fast mover; tag for what the chart actually does).
- **3-c-pattern** (Cup-Completion-Cheat) — Mark Minervini's early-entry setup, adapted from William O'Neil's "cheat," for getting long *inside* a base before the conventional breakout. A **continuation** pattern within an existing [[#stage-analysis|Stage-2]] uptrend (not a bottoming pattern), it runs through four points: **A** — top of the prior advance, where the stock rolls into an intermediate correction (~10–35% deep); **B** — the bounce, recovering ~1/3–1/2 of the decline; **C** — the "cheat," a tight ~5–10% plateau (often with an undercut/shakeout) low in the base; **D** — the buy point, the break above the plateau's high on expanding volume, with a tight stop just below the cheat range. The appeal is reward/risk: entering at the cheat sits you lower with a tighter stop than the full-base pivot. When the plateau forms in the **lower third** of the base it's a **"low cheat"** (deeper, earlier, lower-risk-per-share); a mid-base version is the standard **"cheat."** ("3-C" = the three C-words *Cup-Completion-Cheat*, not three chart points.) *Note:* the "break of a down-trendline drawn across the A-B-C correction" often seen drawn on these charts is the O'Neil charting convention for locating the same pivot — it usually coincides with the plateau-high break, but Minervini's canonical trigger is the plateau high itself. Corpus example: `2002-11-15-JBLU-D-3-c-pattern-minervini` (JetBlue's A-B-C-D cheat into a 130%/11-month run). (Minervini, *Think and Trade Like a Champion* / *Trade Like a Stock Market Wizard*.)
  - **low-cheat** — the [[#3-c-pattern]] variant where the cheat plateau forms in the **lower third** of the base (vs. the mid-base standard "cheat"): a deeper, earlier, lower-price entry with a tighter stop — more attractive reward/risk, but a more aggressive, lower-probability read since less of the base has proven itself. Watch for the **"no handle" launch** — a stock can break straight out of a low cheat without pausing to build a conventional handle near the highs, so the cheat *is* the only entry (wait for the textbook handle and you may never get filled). Corpus example: `2004-09-07-GOOGL-D-low-cheat-minervini` (Google's first post-IPO base, low cheat → no handle → 625% in 40 months).
- **green-line-breakout** — Dr. Eric Wish / Moglen-popularized pattern. A stock breaks above its prior all-time high after consolidating sideways for ≥3 months. The "green line" is the horizontal resistance drawn at the prior ATH; the breakout is the close above it on above-average volume. Premise: ATH means no overhead resistance + the 3-month consolidation is institutional accumulation.
- **faulty-base** — Minervini's term for the anti-pattern: a consolidation that *looks* like a launchpad but resolves the wrong way — instead of breaking out to new highs, it breaks **down** and the decline resumes. Most dangerous when it forms *inside* a downtrend ([[#stage-analysis|Stage 4]]), where it lures buyers anchored to prior highs into a stock that has no business being bought — Stage-4 bases break down far more often than they recover. The tell is the failure itself (a breakout that doesn't come, a breakdown that does), reinforced by [[#relative-weakness]] and a still-bearish MA stack. The constructive mirror of a [[#vcp]] / proper base. Corpus example: `2015-08-15-GPRO-W-faulty-base-minervini` (GoPro's mid-2015 base that broke down on the way from ~$98 to ~$9). (Minervini, *Think and Trade Like a Champion*.)
- **loosening-range** — the inverse of a [[#vcp]]: a base that **widens / loosens** near the highs after an extended advance, instead of *tightening* into a low-volatility pivot. The trendlines drawn across it **diverge** (or slope apart) rather than converge; daily ranges get **bigger**, not smaller, and volume stays elevated/erratic rather than drying up. It's a caution-and-avoidance tell — a base loosening rather than tightening signals indecision and distribution risk at the top of a run, the opposite footprint of a stock under [[#quiet-accumulation|quiet accumulation]]. Distinct from [[#faulty-base]] (which *breaks down*): a loosening range hasn't failed yet — it's a *constructively-ambiguous* widening that can still "rebuild" into a proper tight base, but is not buyable while it's loose. Read it as "wait, don't chase." Corpus example: `2026-06-25-INTC-D-loosening-range-jfsrev` (Jeff Sun / @jfsrev — INTC after a +123% run: an 11-week tightening base launched the move, then a 6-week *loosening* range near the highs flagged it off his watchlist). Source: Jeff Sun (jfsrev), X.

#### Ameet Rai's early-entry setups (TraderLion)

From TraderLion's ["3 Early Entry Techniques"](https://traderlion.com/trading-strategies/3-early-entry-techniques-to-improve-win-rate/) (Ameet Rai, 2024). Techniques for getting into a leader *earlier* (and with tighter risk) than the obvious base-pivot breakout. They compose — an alternative pivot can sit inside a Power of 3, which sets up a Launch Pad. All build on the [[#ma-management]] MA framework.

- **alternative-pivot** (alternative early entry pivot) — a risk-management spot *before* the traditional base pivot: a tight multi-day (or multi-week) consolidation that sits **well off the lows of the structure but not so near the highs** that it's just the normal base buy point. Advantages: better reward/risk than the base pivot, less volatility (fewer eyes), easier pyramiding. Entry on the break of the tight period's highs; stop below its lows (typically 3–5% risk). Multiple unfilled gap-ups around these pivots are a strength tell. Corpus examples: `2020-07-13-TSLA-D-alternative-pivot-traderlion`, `2023-05-15-NVDA-W-alternative-pivot-traderlion`, `2024-05-15-FSLR-D-alternative-pivot-traderlion`.
- **power-of-3** — an early-entry setup where a stock goes from trading **below its 10-, 21-, and 50-day MAs to above all three in a single session** (opens below, closes above). Best with a couple days of tight action below the bunched MAs first (gives an alternative-pivot risk point), **horizontal** (not downtrending) MAs, **volume drying up** below the MAs and **expanding** on the reclaim. Enter on the break of the consolidation high (the bunched MAs sit there), or scale in reclaiming each MA through the session; stop below the consolidation lows or on multiple closes back below. Corpus examples: `2024-05-17-PUBM-D-power-of-3-traderlion`, `2024-04-15-OSCR-D-power-of-3-traderlion`.
- **launch-pad** — the follow-on to a [[#power-of-3]] (but doesn't require one): once price has pushed up through the MAs, those same MAs that were *resistance* should now act as *support*. Premise: the institution that showed its hand on the reclaim keeps building over the next days/weeks, defending the MAs. Enter on multi-day support at a rising MA, a break above a consolidation high, or a short-term pattern (inside day); stop below recent lows (3–5%) or multi-day closes below the MAs. Needs volume + an upside move to *confirm*. Corpus examples: `2024-05-15-BMO-D-launch-pad-traderlion`, `2024-05-15-NMRK-D-launch-pad-traderlion`.
- **high-volume-close** (HVC) — an entry technique for buying *into* a gap (vs. basing setups above): a stock breaks to new highs on a positive earnings/news reaction. Characteristics: **major gap up (10%+)**, **big volume** (highest in 1 year / since IPO / ever — HV1 / HVIPO / HVE), a **strong closing range (>75%)**, and upward fundamental revisions. Three entries: (1) near **volume support** from the gap day, (2) near the **high-volume close** of the gap day, (3) near a relevant lower-timeframe MA. Exits flip the entries (stop below day-1 volume support / the day-1 low, below the HVC, or below a relevant intraday MA). Corpus examples: `2024-04-25-SNAP-D+5m-high-volume-close-traderlion`, `2024-01-22-SMCI-D-high-volume-close-traderlion`, `2024-04-30-TMDX-D+5m-high-volume-close-traderlion`.

### Classic chart patterns (Edwards & Magee / O'Neil tradition)

The textbook swing-trading patterns from TraderLion's ["9 Best Swing Trading Patterns"](https://traderlion.com/technical-analysis/swing-trading-patterns/) overview (Ameet Rai, 2025). These are the broadly-known formations the momentum/CANSLIM tradition inherited from classical technical analysis. Each is either **trend-following**, a **reversal** (signals a trend change), or a **continuation** (trend resumes after a pause).

- **cup-and-handle** — *bullish continuation*. A rounded "U"-shaped cup (consolidation that declines, stabilizes, recovers to prior highs) followed by a small sideways-to-slightly-down **handle**, resolving on a breakout above the prior highs. Volume fades into the cup bottom, builds up the right side, contracts through the handle, spikes on breakout. (O'Neil's classic; same shape as `cup-with-handle`, which is the corpus's older Kell/Schmidt-adjacent tag for it.) **Dan Zanger's quantification** ([chartpattern.com](https://www.chartpattern.com/cup-handle.cfm)): the cup is a 20–35% correction from the highs over ~8–12 weeks after a powerful advance; the **handle should form ~5% below the old high** — much lower than that signals a defective, higher-risk setup — and drifts sideways-to-down for ~4 days to 3 weeks; the **buy is as the stock emerges into new highs at the top of the handle, not at the old high point**. Corpus example: `1999-03-12-CMVT-D-cup-and-handle-zanger` (Zanger's own "most perfect cup and handle" example).
- **head-and-shoulders** — *bearish reversal*. Three peaks — left shoulder, higher **head**, right shoulder level with the left — joined by a **neckline** across the two intervening lows. Confirmed on a **break below the neckline**; signals the end of an uptrend. **Zanger's volume signature** ([chartpattern.com](https://www.chartpattern.com/head-shoulders.cfm)): volume follows price up on the **left shoulder**, the **head** forms on *diminished* volume, the **right shoulder** is lighter still, and volume *increases on the neckline breakdown* — the declining-volume-into-each-peak sequence is the tell that demand is exhausting. Corpus example: `2000-11-28-BRCD-D-head-and-shoulders-zanger` (Brocade's textbook top, labeled LS/H/RS, breaking down on record volume).
- **inverse-head-and-shoulders** — *bullish reversal* (mirror of head-and-shoulders). Left-shoulder trough, lower **head**, higher right shoulder, with a neckline across the two intervening peaks. Confirmed on a **break above the neckline on strong volume**; signals the end of a downtrend. **Zanger's volume signature** ([chartpattern.com](https://www.chartpattern.com/inverted-head-shoulders.cfm)): the **left shoulder** comes on increased volume; the **head** on lighter volume (buyers less aggressive); the rally off the head shows *greater* volume than the rally off the left shoulder; the **right shoulder** the *lightest* volume of all; then a **big volume spike** confirms the neckline break. (Note the neckline can slope *down* across the rally peaks — the buy is the trendline break.) Corpus example: `1999-07-16-NITE-D-inverse-head-and-shoulders-zanger` (Knight/Trimark, ls/h/rs labeled, buy on the descending-neckline break).
- **ascending-triangle** — *bullish continuation*. Horizontal **resistance** (flat top) repeatedly tested, plus an **ascending support** trendline of higher lows. Rising lows = increasing buying interest pressing against resistance until breakout. (Also a `base_shape` in the corpus; as a *pattern* the triangle IS the setup.)
- **descending-triangle** — *bearish continuation*. Horizontal **support** (flat bottom) repeatedly tested, plus a **descending resistance** trendline of lower highs. Weaker rallies = diminishing demand until a breakdown through support. (Zanger's prose matches this textbook read — but note his article's *example chart* doesn't: `1999-02-12-CMGI-D-descending-trendline-break-zanger` is a *bullish* break of a descending resistance line, tagged as a [[#descending-trendline-break]] instead. A reminder to classify the chart, not the article heading.)
- **descending-trendline-break** — *bullish*. Price rides up under a **falling resistance trendline** (drawn across recent lower highs) and the entry triggers as price **crosses up** through that line — a trend-resumption buy on a stock that was consolidating under descending resistance. Not a triangle (no flat support floor) and not bearish; the falling line is just the lid being lifted. (Surfaced from Dan Zanger's CMGI example, which he filed under "descending triangle" but which actually breaks *up* — [chartpattern.com](https://www.chartpattern.com/descending-triangle.cfm).) The mirror-ish bullish cousin of the bearish descending-triangle breakdown.
- **symmetrical-triangle** — *continuation* ("area of indecision"). Two converging trendlines — **descending tops** and **ascending bottoms**, each lower top and higher bottom shallower than the last — coiling toward an apex. Volume diminishes through the formation, then it resolves, usually **exploding out on heavy volume in the direction of the prior trend** (symmetrical triangles overwhelmingly resolve with the trend, so it's a continuation, not a reversal). Buy the breakout. Distinct from `ascending-triangle` (flat top) and `descending-triangle` (flat bottom) — the symmetrical has *both* lines sloping. (Dan Zanger, [chartpattern.com](https://www.chartpattern.com/symmetrical-triangle.cfm).) Corpus example: `1999-02-12-DCLK-D-symmetrical-triangle-zanger`.
- **double-bottom** — *bullish reversal* ("W"). First low, rally to an interim peak (the **neckline**), then a retest of the first low (or slightly lower) that holds — sellers exhausted. Confirmed on a **break above the neckline**. (Also a `base_shape`; the strongest variant is the higher-low double bottom — see Nick Schmidt's pre-character-change base shapes.)
- **double-top** — *bearish reversal* ("M"). First top, pullback to support (the **neckline**), second top near/just above the first that fails, then a **break below the neckline** (often on rising volume).
- **triple-top** — *bearish reversal*. Three peaks at roughly the same resistance level, separated by two intervening troughs (less common than the double-top, slower to form). Confirmed on a **break below support** — the lowest intervening trough — on rising volume. The three-peak cousin of the [[#double-top]]; demand repeatedly rejected at the same price. (StockCharts ChartSchool.) Corpus examples: `1999-11-19-ROK-D-triple-top-stockcharts`, `1998-12-30-CHB-D-triple-top-stockcharts`.
- **triple-bottom** — *bullish reversal*. Three troughs at roughly the same support level, separated by two intervening peaks (less common than the double-bottom, slower to form). Confirmed on a **break above resistance** — the highest intervening peak — on rising volume. The three-trough cousin of the [[#double-bottom]] and the mirror of the [[#triple-top]]; supply repeatedly absorbed at the same price. (StockCharts ChartSchool.) Corpus examples: `2000-06-30-ANDW-D-triple-bottom-stockcharts`, `2000-05-31-APC-D-triple-bottom-stockcharts`.
- **rounding-bottom** (saucer bottom) — *bullish reversal*. A long, gently-curved U/saucer base: a decline, a rounded low forming over several weeks (not a sharp V), then a symmetric advance. Volume is **bowl-shaped** — heavy at the decline's start, drying up at the low, expanding into the advance. Confirmed on a **break above the prior reaction high** (resistance at the decline's start) on rising volume. A long-term pattern best read on the weekly. (StockCharts ChartSchool.) Corpus examples: `1998-05-01-BBY-W-rounding-bottom-stockcharts`, `1999-02-01-AMGN-W-rounding-bottom-stockcharts`.
- **rounding-top** (inverted-saucer / dome top) — *bearish reversal*; the mirror of [[#rounding-bottom]]. A long, gently-curved inverted-U: an advance, a rounded **dome** forming gradually over weeks (not a sharp climax spike), then a symmetric decline. The slow roll is the tell — momentum bleeds out quietly rather than reversing violently, and volume often **fades into the top and stays light** as price rolls over (the absence of a final high-volume push is itself the warning — contrast the [[#climax-top]], which tops on the *heaviest* volume of the move). A long-term pattern, best read on the weekly. Corpus example: `2026-06-23-PLTR-W-stage-4-decline-burry` (Michael Burry — PLTR's low-volume rounded top rolling into a Stage-4 decline).
- **broadening-top** (megaphone / broadening formation) — *bearish reversal*. The inverse of a contracting triangle: two **diverging** trendlines — successively **higher highs and lower lows** — fanning out around a level, so the range *widens* into the top. The expanding swings reflect rising volatility, emotional/indecisive two-sided action, and distribution at the end of an advance (each new high attracts buyers who are then trapped as price whipsaws lower). Hard to trade cleanly *because* it's so volatile; classically marks an unsustainable, sentiment-driven top. The broadening *bottom* (mirror) is rarer and less reliable. (Edwards & Magee / Schabacker; Bulkowski.) Corpus example: `2026-06-25-BTCUSD-W-multi-phase-trend-techcharts` (Aksel Kibar / Tech Charts — a labeled broadening top at the BTC highs preceding the channel breakdown).
- **bump-and-run-reversal** (BARR) — *bearish reversal*. Three phases: a **lead-in** where price rises along a moderate (~30°) trendline; a **bump** where speculative excess accelerates the advance into a much steeper (~45°+) angle, lifting price far above the lead-in line (rising to ~2× the lead-in height); then the **run**, where the bump fails and price breaks back below the lead-in trendline and falls. Marks an unsustainable, sentiment-driven top. (Bulkowski; StockCharts ChartSchool.) Corpus examples: `2000-06-05-INTL-D-bump-and-run-reversal-stockcharts`, `2000-06-05-LVLT-D-bump-and-run-reversal-stockcharts`.
- **measured-move** — *continuation*. A three-part move whose second leg runs roughly the **same length** as the first, with a corrective leg between. **Bullish:** advance → pullback → equal advance. **Bearish:** decline → corrective rally → equal decline. The symmetry is the point — the first leg's height, applied at the start of the second, projects the target. Often read on the weekly for major moves. (StockCharts ChartSchool.) Corpus examples (bull): `1999-12-20-INTC-W-measured-move-stockcharts`, `2000-03-15-WMT-W-measured-move-stockcharts`; (bear): `2000-10-01-XIRC-D-measured-move-stockcharts`, `2000-07-31-WY-D-measured-move-stockcharts`.
- **flag** — *continuation*. A sharp near-vertical **pole**, then a small rectangular **flag** drifting slightly *against* the trend (bull flags slope down, bear flags slope up). Volume high on the pole, contracts in the flag, expands on resumption. (Caveat: charts filed under "Flags" in the source overview often read as horizontal `flat-base`/`range` consolidations rather than classic poled flags — tag for what's actually drawn.) Zanger frames flags as a brief pause in a dynamic stock right after a big quick move, with **parallel** trendlines slanting against the trend and the buy on the break of the flag's trendline ([chartpattern.com](https://www.chartpattern.com/flags-pennants.cfm)). Corpus example: `1999-10-29-ASKJ-D-bull-flag-zanger` (a clean `bull-flag` — pole off the lows, downward flag, buy the break).
- **pennant** — *continuation*. Same idea as a [[#flag]] (a pause right after a sharp pole), but the consolidation is shaped like a small **symmetrical triangle** — *converging* trendlines rather than the flag's parallel ones — and is typically smaller in volatility and duration. Volume contracts in the pennant and increases on the breakout; resolves in the direction of the prior move. (Dan Zanger, [chartpattern.com](https://www.chartpattern.com/flags-pennants.cfm).) Relate: a pennant is to a [[#symmetrical-triangle]] what a flag is to a [[#range-consolidation]] — the same shape, but small and poled.
- **range-consolidation** — *continuation* (a `flat-base` / rectangle). Sideways action between a horizontal **support** and **resistance**, volume contracting within the range, a volume spike on the breakout. Synonymous with the corpus's `flat-base` / `range` tags.
- **darvas-box** — Nicolas Darvas's adaptation of the Edwards-&-Magee / Schabacker **rectangle**: a flat horizontal range ("box") between support and resistance. The method: buy the breakout of the box's top with a stop just under the box, then trail up box-by-box as price steps higher (Darvas paired it with volume confirmation and growing fundamentals). Same *structure* as [[#range-consolidation]] / rectangle — the Darvas framing adds the box-stacking trade rules. Corpus example: `2026-06-13-AVRE-D-darvas-box-techcharts` (Aksel Kibar / Tech Charts).

### Pradeep Bonde's Episodic Pivot variants + Momentum Bursts

From TraderLion's [Bonde EP podcast](https://traderlion.com/podcast/pradeep-bonde-episodic-pivots/) (2025). The base `episodic-pivot` (above) is a high-volume gap-up on a catalyst that forces a rapid revaluation. Bonde distinguishes several **EP variants** (tagged alongside `episodic-pivot`) plus a separate short-term setup:

- **episodic-pivot / delayed-reaction** — when an EP gaps up *too much* to buy safely at the open, wait for a post-gap pullback/consolidation and enter on the breakout of the new range. Lower-risk entry with a clean stop; avoids the day-one shakeout. (Tags: `delayed-reaction`, `pullback-entry`, `low-risk-entry`.)
- **episodic-pivot / 9-million-volume** — a breakout where a stock trades **9M+ shares in a day for the first time**, signaling massive institutional interest. Extreme volume confirms demand; enter near the breakout with a stop below the day's low. (Tags: `9-million-volume`, `institutional-interest`.)
- **episodic-pivot / story-based** — driven by **narrative/speculation** rather than earnings. "Hot money" rotates into a trending sector; recognize early, enter on volume breakouts, take profits fast (these fade quickly but can run 300-400%). High-risk by nature. (Tags: `story-based`, `speculation`, `high-risk`.)
- **momentum-burst** — *distinct from EPs*. A stock in a strong uptrend forms a **tight consolidation**, then breaks out for a sharp **3-5 day move of ~8-20%** before cooling off. Ideal: high-RS leaders, higher-priced ($100+) names in strong sectors, when the market favors breakouts. Often repeats — burst → consolidate → burst. (Bonde's stock-selection overlay is the **MAGNA 53 + CAP 10×10** checklist; see Nick Schmidt's method writeups (nickschmidt.so) if/when that gets captured.)

More EP variants from TradeZella's [Bonde EP writeup](https://www.tradezella.com/strategies/episode-pivot-strategy) (2025), framing the EP family around three core elements — **neglect** (dead range for months), **new catalyst** (earnings surprise, turnaround, theme, or extreme volume), **rapid repricing** (large gap + strong follow-through on huge volume), targeting 20–300% moves in 10–20 trading days:

- **episodic-pivot / classical-growth** (`growth-ep`) — the canonical EP: a real growth catalyst (wants **triple-digit, 100%+ sales growth** confirmation). Entry market-on-open or within the first 5–10 min; stop below the opening-range low; trail under daily swing lows. Corpus: `2024-02-26-ROOT-D-...`, `2024-01-19-SMCI-D-...-tradezella`. (Tags: `growth-ep`, `catalyst-earnings`.)
- **episodic-pivot / turnaround** (`turnaround-ep`) — catalyst is a *recovery* (new CEO, business inflection) in a beaten-down name. **Trends longer** than pure growth EPs (the improvement plays out over multiple quarters), so the stop is managed *less aggressively* — trailed beneath significant swing lows. Corpus: `2023-06-01-ANF-D-...` (ANF's ~700–800% turnaround). (Tags: `turnaround-ep`.)
- **episodic-pivot / delayed-reaction-short** — the short side of [[#episodic-pivot--delayed-reaction]]: after a *negative* catalyst, wait for the relief bounce to fail and enter as the delayed weakness appears. (Tags: `delayed-reaction`, `short`.)
- **sugar-babies** — Bonde's term for **recurring EP-style names** — habitual runners that produce **40–50% bursts in 5–6 days** repeatedly. Kept as a curated watchlist of stocks that EP again and again, rather than a single setup. (Tags: `sugar-babies`, `recurring`.)

### Oliver Kell's Cycle of Price Action patterns

These belong to the [[#frameworks|cycle-of-price-action]] framework — see below for how they sequence.

- **reversal-extension** — the *precursor* to a bottom. Price extends sharply away from declining MAs (often on capitulation volume), then snaps back up into the MAs. Kell frames this as "not necessarily a playable setup but more of a change in mindset" — a recognition that the downtrend may be ending, not a tradeable entry. The actual playable trade waits for the subsequent Wedge Pop (or EMA Crossback / Base 'n Break). Often confirmed by alignment with a higher-timeframe support area. (Stage: bottoming.)
- **wedge-pop** — Kell's "Money Pattern." After a Reversal Extension, price consolidates beneath flattening MAs forming a tighter range, then breaks up through the MAs on accumulation volume. The "pop" is the trade through the first pivot above the consolidation. Confirms a *bottom*, not a continuation. (Stage: bottom confirmation.)
- **ema-crossback** — after a Wedge Pop confirms a bottom, price pulls back to retest the MAs (typically 10/20 EMA), which act as support. The second tradeable entry in the cycle — add to a position or initiate one if the Wedge Pop was missed. (Stage: confirmation after Wedge Pop.)
- **base-n-break** — after EMA Crossback support holds, price drifts sideways ~2 weeks letting the MAs catch up, then breaks out of the tight base. Often occurs multiple times within a sustained uptrend. The "ride the trend" entry. (Stage: trend continuation.)
- **exhaustion-extension** — signals the end of an uptrend. Euphoric blowoff extending price well above MAs on multiple timeframes, then reversing lower as sellers appear. The further into an uptrend, the more frequently extensions appear. Some stocks top without exhaustion — via tightening then a Wedge Drop instead. (Stage: topping.)
- **wedge-drop** — bearish mirror of the Wedge Pop. After an Exhaustion Extension (or tight topping action), price falls through the MAs with an influx of sellers, confirming the reversal. A new downtrend begins. (Stage: downtrend confirmation.)
- **ema-crossback-down** — bearish mirror of EMA Crossback. After Wedge Drop puts price below the MAs, the first rally back into the declining MAs meets *resistance* (rather than support), pushing the stock lower. (Stage: downtrend continuation.)
- **base-n-break-down** — bearish mirror of Base 'n Break. Price builds small bases as it cycles downward; MAs act as resistance, sending the stock lower at each break. Eventually a Reversal Extension restarts the cycle. (Stage: late downtrend.)
- **parabolic-short** — Qullamaggie's mean-reversion short. A stock goes vertical — up 50-100%+ (larger cap) or 300-1000%+ (small cap) in days/weeks, up 3-5+ days in a row — stretching far above its MAs into a blowoff. Short the *first intraday failure*: opening-range lows (1-/5-min), or wait for the first crack, a bounce back into VWAP, and a fail at VWAP (= first red 1-/5-min candle into VWAP). Stop tight at the highs of the day or a VWAP reclaim. Target the **10- and 20-day MAs**, where these stocks usually bounce. Tradeable cousin of Kell's [[#exhaustion-extension]] — but where exhaustion-extension is "a change in mindset," the parabolic short has *specific entry/stop mechanics*. (Qullamaggie's named setup; see Qullamaggie's 3 timeless setups method.)
- **parabolic-long** — the bullish side of the parabolic. Enter on opening-range highs, the first green 5-min candle, or any upside range break as a stretched stock keeps accelerating; these can bounce/extend 50-100% in a few days. Same exhaustion dynamics, played long into the blowoff — higher risk, fast. (Qullamaggie's named setup.)

## Signals

A signal is an individual bar/range observation, often single-bar — distinct from patterns (multi-bar formations). A chart can carry zero or many.

- **key-reversal-bar** — a strong-volume reversal bar (often a gap-up that closes near lows) after a mature trend. Frequently signals the start of a topping base or the end of an intermediate move.
- **positive-expectation-breaker** — a positive close on a bar that broke down on the prior bar, contrary to expected continuation. Often a reversal signal at base lows.
- **squat** (fake break / fake-breakout) — a breakout or breakdown that **fails to follow through** and reverses, revealing the move was a trap. Named for price "squatting" instead of running after it clears a level — the absence of the expected continuation *is* the signal. A breakdown below support that squats and reverses up is the bullish version (a shakeout of weak hands / stops, the supply at the level absorbed rather than overwhelming); a breakout above resistance that squats and fails is the bearish version. Highest-quality on a leader with strong relative strength — RS turning up at the failure point confirms it. The trade is the *reclaim* (re-entering the broken level) with risk against the squat extreme. Close kin to [[#positive-expectation-breaker]] (the established term for "broke down, then closed positive against expected continuation") and to a failed [[#role-reversal]] retest. (Elite Swing Traders / @1ChartMaster, X, 2026; the term traces to the market-profile / TradeStation lineage.) Corpus example: `2026-04-20-MU-W-positive-expectation-breaker-1chartmaster`.
- **gap-and-go** — a gap up at open followed by sustained higher action through the day. Distinct from a fading gap.
- **opening-range-breakout** — intraday breakout from the first N-minute range (commonly 5min ORB).
- **pocket-pivot** — within-base volume surge on a positive day, without a full breakout. Pre-breakout strength signal. (O'Neil / Morales framework.) **Ameet Rai's (TraderLion) definition:** the current up-day's volume must be **larger than any down-day's volume in the prior 10 days** — an institutional accumulation footprint inside a base or uptrend. Best when it emerges from a constructive base, the candle closes in the upper half of its range, and it comes in clusters of 3+. Not an automatic buy — a sign to watch the stock closely. Corpus examples: `2023-12-15-SMCI-D-pocket-pivot-traderlion`, `2023-11-15-GTLB-D-pocket-pivot-traderlion`.
- **quiet-accumulation** — Ameet Rai's (TraderLion) term for **tight price action + a volume dry-up** marking institutions building a position quietly. A fund manager filling a large order buys just enough to hold price in a narrow range without pushing it out (which would raise their average cost and attract other buyers), so the tape shows tightening ranges, similar week-after-week closes, and *declining* volume — just enough demand to hold price up, plus a lack of willing sellers. The dry-up is the tell: the *same* tight action on *high* volume is suspect (likely two-sided churn / distribution), so its breakout is less reliable. Cousin of [[#tight-action]] and [[#pocket-pivot]] (footprints of the same institutional buying); `volume-dry-up` is the companion descriptive tag. Corpus examples: `2023-11-15-NVDA-W-quiet-accumulation-traderlion`, `2023-04-15-CAMT-W-quiet-accumulation-traderlion`, `2023-07-15-AMR-W-quiet-accumulation-traderlion`.
- **distribution** — the bearish mirror of [[#quiet-accumulation]]: institutions *unloading* a large position into a top or a decline. The footprints are the opposite of accumulation — supply showing up on strength (heavy-volume up-bars that close weak), a rounded/low-volume top where demand quietly leaves, and crucially **volume that fails to recover as price falls** (no willing buyers stepping in to defend the move). Read it as the absence of the accumulation tell: where a [[#volume-dry-up]] during *tight basing* is bullish (quiet buying), a volume dry-up *into a top and through a decline* is bearish (the demand is simply gone). Often the volume signature under a [[#rounding-top]] or a [[#stage-analysis|Stage-3→4]] roll. Corpus example: `2026-06-23-PLTR-W-stage-4-decline-burry` (Michael Burry — "volume fell into the top and still has not recovered as it falls").
- **wedge-pop** (as a signal) — resolution of a wedge formation with a strong upward break.
- **expansion-down-bar** — wide-range down bar with increased volume, often after a failed test.
- **tight-action** — bars with overlapping/narrow ranges, often pre-breakout. Plural cousin of inside-day. **Ameet Rai's (TraderLion) "tight areas":** a stretch where price drifts sideways-to-slightly-lower and the high-to-low range **constricts relative to recent action**, most constructive when volume dries up alongside — a balance of supply and demand that in strong stocks signals accumulation and precedes a move, offering a low-risk entry as price emerges through the pivot at the top of the range. The building block of Minervini's [[#vcp]] and O'Neil's "tight weekly action." Deepvue's **RMV (Relative Measured Volatility)** indicator objectifies it (reads ~0 when tight, ~100 when expanded). Corpus examples: `2023-07-10-SHOP-D-tight-action-traderlion` (with RMV), `2023-09-15-CEIX-D-tight-action-traderlion`.
- **inside-day** — a bar whose high and low are inside the prior bar's range. Volatility contraction.
- **3-weeks-tight** — three consecutive weekly bars with closes within ~1.5% of each other. (O'Neil framework.) Pre-breakout signal on the weekly.
- **ma-reclaim** — price closes back above a previously-lost moving average.
- **volume-surge** — daily/weekly volume meaningfully above the 50-period average.
- **downside-reversal** — open up, close near lows on a single bar, often with volume.
- **all-time-high-breakout** — price closes above its prior all-time high. Distinct from a base-breakout (which clears recent resistance) because there are no trapped overhead sellers above the level. Underlies green-line-breakout but valid on its own.
- **positive-divergence** — a momentum oscillator (MACD, PPO, RSI, Accum/Dist, OBV) making **higher lows while price makes lower lows**. The internal strength diverging from the falling price foreshadows a bullish reversal — selling pressure is waning even as price prints new lows. A confirmation tell on bottoming patterns rather than a standalone entry. Corpus examples: `1999-04-09-RDC-D-falling-wedge-stockcharts` (MACD), `2000-06-30-ANDW-D-triple-bottom-stockcharts` (Accum/Dist).
- **negative-divergence** — the bearish mirror: an oscillator making **lower highs while price makes higher highs**. The fading momentum behind new price highs foreshadows a bearish reversal — demand is exhausting even as price prints new highs. A confirmation tell on topping patterns. Corpus example: `2002-01-02-DELL-D-rising-wedge-stockcharts` (PPO "Neg Diverge").
- **relative-weakness** — a stock materially *underperforming the general market* over the same window — the bearish mirror of relative strength. Minervini's rule of thumb: **avoid stocks that correct more than roughly 2.5–3× the decline of the general market** (a name falling hard while the market holds or rises is failing on its own merits, not just riding a market pullback). A disqualifying filter independent of the chart pattern — a tidy-looking base on a relatively-weak name is still a stay-away. The companion read to [[#faulty-base]] / [[#stage-analysis|Stage 4]]. Corpus example: `2015-08-15-GPRO-W-faulty-base-minervini` (down 61% while the NASDAQ was up 10% over the same period). (Minervini, *Think and Trade Like a Champion*.)
- **relative-strength** — a stock *outperforming the general market* over the same window — the bullish mirror of [[#relative-weakness]], and most diagnostic **at a market turn**. A true leader bottoms first, holds up better through a decline, and turns up *ahead of* the index; its relative-strength line (price ÷ a market proxy) makes a higher low and rises even while price is still basing. Minervini's caution (the section *How to Correctly Use Relative Strength*): RS is **outperformance vs. the market, not "what went up the most in absolute terms"** — the naive version points you at extended late-stage movers; the correct version, read at a market bottom, points you at the *next* leaders being accumulated ahead of the crowd. Pairs with a [[#character-change|Stage 1→2 character change]] (the leader emerges from its base on volume as the market low forms). Corpus example: `2002-10-10-AMZN-W-relative-strength-minervini` (Amazon's RS leading as it bottomed on the exact day the market bottomed, Oct 10 2002, then ran ~1700% in 16 months). (Minervini, *Think and Trade Like a Champion*.)
- **support-resistance** — a horizontal price level the market repeatedly reacts to. As **resistance** it caps rallies; once broken and reclaimed it flips to **support**, where buyers step in on pullbacks (and vice versa for a broken support becoming resistance). The more times a level is touched and holds, the more significant it is. The trade is a low-risk entry near the level with a tight stop just beyond — the appeal is the objective, unambiguous invalidation point (everyone sees the same price), versus a subjective diagonal trendline. Corpus example: `2026-06-12-PLTR-D-support-resistance-investingluc`. The StockCharts ChartSchool set adds the sub-types: `support-level` / `resistance-level` (which side is being tested), `support-resistance-zone` (S/R as a *band* rather than an exact line — better for large/volatile ranges), and [[#role-reversal]] (a level flipping function).
- **role-reversal** — once a horizontal S/R level breaks, it tends to switch function: broken **resistance becomes support**, and broken **support becomes resistance** (trapped participants defend the old level on the retest — buyers who missed the breakout, or holders selling to "get out even"). A *successful* retest of the flipped level is a high-quality, low-risk entry confirming the breakout held; a *failed* retest confirms the breakdown. The same level doing two jobs at different times. Corpus examples: `1998-02-20-NDX-W-role-reversal-stockcharts` (resistance→support), `2000-03-10-PSFT-D-role-reversal-stockcharts` (support→resistance).

The next two are **ohiain's leadership-pullback concepts** (from his X writeups, 2026-06) — *areas of interest* on proven leaders rather than mechanical entry signals; the entry is always confirmed on a lower timeframe (15/30-min pivot reclaim) with risk against the low. Tagged on charts alongside `pullback-entry` / `low-risk-entry`.

- **9-week-ma-hold** — (ohiain) a market leader pulling back into the **9-week MA** (≈ the daily 50 EMA) after a base breakout and finding support there. Not an entry by itself — the *area* where you watch the reaction (15/30-min undercut-and-reclaims, VWAP reclaims, higher-TF failed breakdowns, higher lows, buyers defending weekly support) for a low-risk continuation entry. The **reaction matters more than the level**: a clean slice with heavy selling that can't reclaim is a warning; sellers pushing into it and buyers immediately defending is the tell. Corpus examples: `2026-06-13-{INTC,AMKR,AEVA,OUST}-W-9-week-ma-hold-ohiain`. Close kin to [[#first-pullback]].
- **first-pullback** — (ohiain) buying the **first (or second) pullback after a base breakout** rather than the breakout itself. The breakout is "the market making a statement" (proves demand, institutions show their hand); the pullback is "the market asking a question" — are buyers willing to defend the area? On a proven leader (strong RS vs. market + sector), wait for **tightness** (a tight range while volume dries up = sellers exhausting, buyers absorbing supply) and **volume contraction** on the pullback after expansion on the breakout, then enter on a lower-timeframe pivot reclaim with risk against the low. The lowest-risk point to join an established trend. Corpus example: `2026-06-13-AMKR-D-first-pullback-ohiain`. Close kin to [[#9-week-ma-hold]].

## Trendlines

A **trendline** is a foundational primitive — a straight line connecting two or more price points that then acts as dynamic support or resistance. Distinct from a horizontal [[#support-resistance]] level (which is flat) — a trendline is *sloped*. Many patterns are built from trendlines (wedges, channels, triangles, flags), but the line itself is the base concept. (Source: StockCharts ChartSchool [trend lines](https://chartschool.stockcharts.com/table-of-contents/chart-analysis/trend-lines).)

- **up-trendline** — a *rising* line drawn along the **higher reaction lows** of an uptrend; acts as **support**. Pullbacks to it find buyers, so it both defines the trend and offers lower-risk entries on tests of it. A decisive close below warns the uptrend may be ending. Corpus: `2000-03-03-VRSN-D-up-trendline-stockcharts`, `1999-02-03-YHOO-D-up-trendline-stockcharts`, `2000-02-11-EMC-W-up-trendline-stockcharts`, `2000-10-20-SPX-W-up-trendline-stockcharts` (major/index trend).
- **down-trendline** — a *falling* line drawn along the **lower reaction highs** of a downtrend; acts as **resistance**. Rallies to it meet sellers and fail; a decisive close above warns the downtrend may be ending. Corpus: `2000-03-10-KO-D-down-trendline-stockcharts`, `2000-03-03-WMT-D-down-trendline-stockcharts`, `2002-12-20-AMZN-W-down-trendline-stockcharts`.
- **valid-trendline** — what makes a trendline trustworthy: **at least three touches** (two draw it, a third confirms), **well-spaced in time** (not bunched), each a genuine reaction off the line, with a **sustainable slope** (too steep breaks quickly). The more well-spaced touches it holds, the more weight a break carries. Corpus: `2000-03-03-MSFT-D-valid-trendline-stockcharts`.
- **log-vs-arithmetic** (scale) — trendlines depend on the price axis. **Arithmetic** = equal *dollar* spacing; **semi-log** = equal *percent* spacing. For large-percentage moves the two diverge sharply, so a line drawn on one scale won't map onto the other (touch points, slope, and break level all shift). ChartSchool's guidance: use **semi-log** for trendlines on big moves, since markets move in percentage terms. Corpus: `2002-12-20-AMZN-W-trendline-scale-stockcharts`, `2000-02-11-EMC-W-trendline-scale-stockcharts`.

## Gaps

A **gap** is an empty space on a chart where price jumps from one bar to the next without trading in between — a supply/demand imbalance, usually from an overnight news/earnings event. An **up gap** opens above the prior bar's high; a **down gap** opens below the prior bar's low. What makes a gap meaningful is *where in a trend it occurs* and *the volume behind it* — the four-type taxonomy below. (Source: StockCharts ChartSchool [gaps and gap analysis](https://chartschool.stockcharts.com/table-of-contents/chart-analysis/gaps-and-gap-analysis); relates to [[#gap-and-go]] and the gap-up that drives an [[#episodic-pivot]].)

- **common-gap** — a small gap *within* a trading range / congestion, on no catalyst and ordinary volume. The least significant type; carries little predictive value and usually **fills** within days. Noise to trade around, not a signal. Corpus: `2023-01-13-MSFT-D-common-gap-stockcharts`.
- **breakaway-gap** — a gap at the **start of a new move**: price gaps *out of* a range/base, clearing resistance (or support) on a **volume surge**. The conviction confirms the breakout and breakaway gaps often **don't fill** (the space becomes support). Begins a trend. Corpus: `2023-05-31-NFLX-D-breakaway-gap-stockcharts`.
- **runaway-gap** — (a.k.a. continuation / measuring gap) a gap **in the middle of an established trend**, on a **volume increase**, as momentum accelerates. Confirms the trend has further to run (often ~halfway through the move); doesn't fill while the trend persists. Corpus: `2023-04-14-META-D-runaway-gap-stockcharts`.
- **exhaustion-gap** — a gap **near the end of a trend**, on **climactic, well-above-average volume** (the last buyers/sellers piling in), that quickly stalls, reverses, and **fills** within days. The aftermath distinguishes it from a runaway gap: runaway holds and continues; exhaustion fails. Often marks a top/bottom. Corpus: `2022-09-09-WMT-D-exhaustion-gap-stockcharts`. (Intro/both-directions example: `2023-03-20-HD-D-gap-types-stockcharts`.)
- **gap-fill** (gap-support) — a *setup*, not a gap type: price leaves an **upside gap**, later **pulls back and fills it** (trades back down through the empty space), the **filled gap holds as support**, and the stock **continues higher** off it. The trade is the bounce off the filled-gap level with risk just below it. A special case of [[#role-reversal]] applied to a gap — the gap zone flips from a launch point to a support floor on the retest — and the bull-side counterpart to "gaps often get filled" (an [[#exhaustion-gap]] fills *and fails*; a gap-fill-and-hold fills *and resumes*). **Field caveat (Prof / @TheProfInvestor):** gaps "don't fill to the penny every time" — treat the gap as a *zone* to watch the reaction in, not an exact price; the textbook teaches the pattern, the tape teaches how loose the fill runs. Best as a quick mean-reversion bounce trade on indices (SPY/QQQ), often coinciding with a fast MA (e.g. the 9 EMA) at the same level. Corpus examples: `2026-06-17-SPY-4H-gap-fill-theprofinvestor`, `2026-06-17-QQQ-4H-gap-fill-theprofinvestor`.

## Frameworks

A framework names a *sequence* or *cycle* of patterns rather than a single chart shape — it organizes the relationships between patterns.

- **cycle-of-price-action** — Oliver Kell's master framework. Orders his patterns into one upside-and-downside cycle:

  **Reversal Extension → Wedge Pop → EMA Crossback → Base 'n Break → Exhaustion Extension → Wedge Drop → EMA Crossback Down → Base 'n Break Down → (back to Reversal Extension)**

  Each pattern is a checkpoint on the same arc; the framework is the recognition that they're parts of one cycle, not independent setups. Full writeup: the Cycle of Price Action method writeup (TraderLion / Kell) in `methods/`.

- **ma-management** — Ameet Rai's (TraderLion) moving-average trade-management method: use the MA a stock *respects* to read trend and manage a position. **(1) Trend identification** — the slope of the MA gives direction; its steepness gives strength (a rising 10EMA = strong trend, a flat one = weak). **(2) Pick the right MA** — find the one the name historically holds (look left); a 10SMA stop fails on a 20EMA-respecting stock. **(3) Offensive extension sell** — trim a portion into strength when stretched far above the MA (e.g. 10–25%+ above the 10/20EMA; no universal %, it's per-stock). **(4) Defensive close-below exit** — sell on a *break* of the MA, refined by the **two-closes-below rule** (require two consecutive closes below, not a single intraday undercut, to avoid shake-out exits). **(5) Add on an MA hold** — a low-volume retrace to the MA + 2–3 days' consolidation + price compression (inside day) is a buyable continuation, not a blind "buy every touch." Oliver Kell's template: 10EMA, 20EMA, 50SMA, 200SMA. Builds on the corpus's `ma-extension` / `ma-hold` / `ma-respect` / `ma-reclaim` / `trailing-stop` tags. Corpus examples: `2023-11-15-TSLA-D-ma-management-traderlion` (trend ID), `2024-04-01-SPY-D-ma-management-traderlion` (which MA), `2024-02-15-SMCI-D-ma-management-traderlion` (extension sell), `2018-09-15-AMD-D-ma-management-traderlion` (2x-close-below), `2018-07-15-NFLX-D-ma-management-traderlion` (all rules).
- **ipo-lifecycle** — the three-phase model of how the best post-IPO stocks behave, from *The Lifecycle Trade* (Kathy Donnelly, Eric Krull, Kurt Daill, Eve Boboch). Orders a stock's early public life into:

  **IPO Advance Phase (IPO-AP) → Institutional Due Diligence Phase (I-DDP) → Institutional Advance Phase (I-AP)**

  - **IPO-AP** — the sharp post-debut rally on hype + early institutional interest (days–weeks). Trade nimbly, tight stops. Failure mode: IPO Advance Failure (IPO-AF), a reversal that traps late buyers.
  - **I-DDP** — a long consolidation (months–years) as institutions evaluate fundamentals; sideways/choppy, volume dries up, weak hands shaken out. Mostly a no-trade zone; watch for accumulation (higher lows, volume spikes).
  - **I-AP** — breakout from a mature base on heavy volume into a sustained multi-quarter/year uptrend; the highest-probability trades (classic bases backed by volume + earnings).

  Premise: the biggest growth-stock moves often come within ~10 years post-IPO, and knowing the phase tells you to strike, wait, or sit out. Corpus examples (TraderLion's IPO article, 2025): `2020-09-30-PLTR-…`, `2023-06-16-CAVA-…`, `2019-04-19-ZM-…`, `2021-07-29-HOOD-…`, `2010-06-29-TSLA-…-ipo-lifecycle-traderlion` (all in chartsdb).

## Base shapes

For setups built on a base. In the corpus these fold into tags.

## Stage analysis (Weinstein)

**stage-analysis** — Stan Weinstein's framework classifying a stock's position in one of four regimes, read off price and the moving averages (the **30-week MA** is the regime line; the **10-week MA** the faster surf line). The parent framework that `character-change` and `new-momentum-zone` (the Stage 4→1→2 transition) sit inside. For the *actionable method* — the 9-question read, weak-vs-A+ breakout components, investor-vs-trader entries, the 10W-MA surf, exits — see the Stan Weinstein / stageanalysis.net method. The four stages, with their MA signature:

- **stage-1 — basing.** Sideways accumulation after a decline; MAs flatten and intertwine, price oscillates around them.
- **stage-2 — advancing.** The markup phase; price above rising MAs, higher highs and higher lows (MAs stacked, respected on pullbacks). Where trend-following money is made.
- **stage-3 — topping.** Distribution; the advance stalls, MAs flatten and roll over, volatility rises at the high.
- **stage-4 — declining.** The markdown phase; price below falling MAs, lower lows — then the decline exhausts and a new stage-1 base forms.

- Composite transitions use `-to-` (e.g. `stage-1-to-2`, `stage-4-to-1`).
- **full-cycle** — a chart that shows all four stages (and the turn into the next stage 1) in one view. The canonical teaching picture for stage analysis. Tag `stage-analysis` + `full-cycle` + each `stage-N` present. Corpus example: `2026-06-08-SFM-W-stage-analysis` (SFM's full 1→2→3→4→1 on the weekly).
