# Playbook — Character Change (Stage 1→2)

A **playbook** is a repeatable *procedure* plus an *eval rubric* — distinct from the [[glossary]] (which *defines* patterns) and from a chart entry (which is one marked-up *instance*). This one covers the **character change**: catching a stock as it transitions from a long downtrend into the birth of a new uptrend. It is read on the **weekly**, and it is Stage-Analysis-native (the Weinstein Stage 1→2 turn, framed in Nick Schmidt's terms).

It is dual-purpose by design:
- **Prospective (hunting):** walk the steps below to *find* and *mark up* a candidate.
- **Retrospective (eval):** read the same criteria in reverse to *grade* a marked-up chart and give feedback.

They are the same criteria in two directions.

---

## When to use it

- A stock that has been in a **prolonged downtrend** (months to years) and is showing the first evidence the trend has turned. "Dumpster diving" by feel — by the time the setup completes, the downtrend has usually been over for a while.
- **Weekly chart only.** The weekly cuts the noise and shows the real trend; the daily whipsaws you out of these.
- Charts set with **10-week + 30-week SMAs + volume**. (Drop the 40-week; two MAs keep the read clean.) The **30-week is the regime line**; the 10-week is the faster surf line.
- *Not* for stocks already in an established Stage-2 uptrend — that's the [[buying-in-bases]] playbook (the continuation partner). The character change is the *birth*; buying-in-bases is the *continuation*.

## The thesis

A downtrend ends when supply exhausts and institutions begin accumulating. Big funds can't buy quietly, so their footprint shows up as a **volume surge on a strong up-move**, a **higher low** (buyers stepping in at elevated prices), and — the low-risk part — price **reclaiming and respecting a moving average that had been resistance** the whole way down. When those align on the weekly, the odds strongly favor a new Stage-2 trend, and you can enter with tightly-defined risk. The patience is the edge: you let the stock prove the turn before committing.

## Hunt / scan steps

1. **Filter to beaten-down names.** Down materially from highs, long base, was a real leader once. (Screener proxy: negative-to-flat 1yr performance, price near the 52-week low, then flip through candidates on the weekly.)
2. **Look for the W / higher-low base forming.** A higher-low double-bottom gives *weeks of advance notice* — once the second (higher) low is in and climbing on declining volume, put the name on the watchlist *before* the trigger fires.
3. **Watch the 30-week.** The first tell is the 30-week *stops falling and flattens*. That's "wake up," not "buy."
4. **Wait for the trigger cluster:** price closes back above the 30-week + a volume surge (biggest in months, closing strong). By this point pieces 1 (higher low) and 2 (volume) are usually both in.
5. **Take the entry on the first 10-week respect** — the pullback that holds the now-rising 10-week, ideally where broken resistance flips to support (role reversal). Stop just below the MA / recent higher low (Schmidt: 4–8% on the weekly is fine). See your position-sizing rules for share count.

## Annotation checklist (the marks to draw, in order)

Draw these five, left to right — they tell the story:

1. **Higher low** — bracket the base low where price quits making lower lows.
2. **30-week flattens** — arrow on the 30-week where its slope rolls from down to sideways (the first tell).
3. **Volume surge / first close above 30-week** — the trigger candle(s): reclaim of the 30-week on expanding volume. This is *the event*.
4. **First 10-week respect** — the pullback that holds the rising 10-week (the low-risk entry). Note "role reversal" if the broken level flipped to support.
5. **Stage-2 confirmation** — the first base-within-the-trend (optional; it's really the start of [[buying-in-bases]]).

Keep it sparse. The best teaching charts have ~4–5 marks, not fifteen.

## Eval rubric — a lens, not a checklist

> **Read this as a judge's bench notes, not a scorecard.** These are the criteria to *reason against* so grading is grounded and consistent — NOT boxes to tick. Judge the chart: note where a criterion is *partially* met, where a "failure" is actually instructive, and where the chart does something the criteria don't anticipate. **A chart can be excellent while missing a criterion, and weak while checking every box.** Two anchors:
> - *Excellent-but-incomplete:* `2022-11-15-NVDA-W-character-change-trevor` — a real character change that RAN without ever offering the clean 10-week-respect entry. Marking that absence (not forcing a fake #3) is the *correct*, advanced read.
> - *Checks-boxes-but-weak:* a stock extended vertically into its pivot (cf. VELO discussion) — every element "present," but buying it is a knife, not a setup.

Criteria to weigh:

1. **Are Schmidt's three pieces present, and in a sensible order?** Higher low + volume surge + first 10-week respect. First two in either order; third is the entry. *Judge quality, not just presence* — a "volume surge" that's actually even/unremarkable is a weaker piece (note it); a screaming earnings-gap surge is a strong one.
2. **The "looking left" filter (highest-value check).** Did the reclaimed MA *act as resistance on the way down*? "The moving average that had been holding it down is now holding it up." An MA the stock chopped through repeatedly is not relevant; the reclaim of one that capped every rally is the real tell. This is the single most-skipped, most-discriminating criterion — always check it.
3. **Accumulation vs. liquidation on the volume.** Read where the big-volume candle *closes*. A huge spike on a *down* week or a reversal closing weak is liquidation (capitulation), not accumulation — the same-size bar, opposite meaning. A good annotation does NOT mark a capitulation spike as the signal.
4. **Is the entry defensible?** The low-risk entry is the 10-week respect (or the reclaim itself if the stock never pulls back). Is the stop close and structural, or miles away? Buying extended into the pivot fails this even if pieces 1–3 are "there."
5. **Regime honesty.** Is this genuinely a *former* downtrend turning (Stage 4→1→2), or a Stage-2 continuation misfiled here (→ that's [[buying-in-bases]])? Or a Stage-4 bounce that will fail (a faulty base)?

When you grade: name what's strong, what's partial, and the *one thing* that most determines whether it's real. Frame feedback in Schmidt's + Weinstein's vocabulary.

## Common failure modes (what disqualifies or weakens it)

- **Liquidation volume mistaken for accumulation** — the FTX/Qwikster-style capitulation spike is *not* the signal (see BTCUSD, NFLX).
- **Extended into the pivot** — a near-vertical run *into* the reclaim leaves no low-risk entry; the base-buy window has closed.
- **MA not "relevant looking left"** — a reclaim of an MA the stock ignored on the way down is a weak tell.
- **Parabolic base / post-blowoff** — a stock that went vertical then based needs longer digestion; the first pullback is a knife, not a CC entry.
- **Stage-4 faulty base** — a base *inside* a still-downtrending name that breaks *down*, not up. Relative weakness vs. the market is the warning.

## Worked examples (in the corpus)

**MA-reclaim form:** `2009-03-15-CMG-W-stage-1-to-2-trevor` (transition-is-a-zone), `2025-05-15-TAN-W-...` (sector-ETF rotation), `2022-11-15-META-W-...` (screaming-volume, clean 10-week entry).
**Base-breakout form:** `2024-11-15-TWLO-W-stage-1-to-2-breakout-trevor` (horizontal pivot + role-reversal retest), `2023-03-15-DELL-W-...` (+ first base).
**No-clean-entry (the instructive exception):** `2022-11-15-NVDA-W-...` (ran without offering #3).
**Quiet-accumulation variant:** `2023-05-15-CRWD-W-...` (base tightened on the MAs, muted volume).
**With first Stage-2 base stacked:** `2012-11-15-NFLX-W-...`, `2023-01-15-BTCUSD-W-...` (repeating bases).
**Schmidt's own (maturity spectrum):** `2024-10-17-APP-W-character-change-schmidt` (completed), `...PYPL...` (developing), `...PTON...` (very early).
