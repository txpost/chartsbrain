# Playbook — High Tight Flag (O'Neil)

A **playbook** is a repeatable *procedure* plus an *eval rubric* — distinct from the [[glossary]] (which *defines* patterns) and from a chart entry (which is one marked-up *instance*). This one covers the **high tight flag (HTF)**: William O'Neil's rarest and most explosive continuation pattern — a stock that surges **100%+ in only 4–8 weeks** on a genuine new-product catalyst, then consolidates **very tightly for 3–5 weeks** on a volume dry-up before breaking out to extend the move.

It is dual-purpose by design:
- **Prospective (hunting):** walk the steps below to *find* and *mark up* a candidate.
- **Retrospective (eval):** read the same criteria in reverse to *grade* a marked-up chart and give feedback.

They are the same criteria in two directions.

> **The discipline this playbook exists to enforce:** the HTF is *the most over-called pattern in the book*. O'Neil says it appears "only once or twice during a bull market year," yet people tag it on any strong stock that pauses. **Most charts that look like an HTF are not one** — they're a longer Stage-2 base, a flat base, or a `buying-in-bases` continuation. The load-bearing test is the **compact pole** (100%+ in *4–8 weeks*, not a multi-month grind). If the pole isn't there, it isn't an HTF — grade it as whatever it actually is. (This is exactly the call made against `2026-06-22-ICHR-W-range-breakout-shankar-nath`: a real, explosive move, but a multi-month trend, so `range-breakout` / `stage-analysis`, *not* HTF.)

---

## When to use it

- A stock coming off a **compact, near-vertical advance — roughly 100–120%+ in 4–8 weeks** — that is now going sideways in a very tight range. If the move that precedes the flag took months, it is **not** an HTF (that's a Stage-2 base / [[buying-in-bases]]).
- **Weekly chart, log scale**, with a **10-week MA + volume**. (The flag's tightness reads on the weekly; the daily is noise. Log scale matters because these move in multiples.)
- The catalyst is usually a **genuine new product / new-issue re-rating** on a **little-known company** (not the biggest name in its group) with **strong, accelerating fundamentals**. The chart pattern without the catalyst is suspect.
- A **continuation** setup — the HTF pauses an advance that's already underway; it is *not* a bottoming / trend-birth pattern (that's [[character-change]]). It frequently follows a prior base (a cup-with-handle) or **repeats** on the same leader (TASR printed three).
- *Not* for a stock that merely "went up a lot and paused." Rarity is a feature — if you're finding several a month, you're mislabeling.

## The thesis

A new product turns a not-well-known company into a fast mover — 100%+ in a handful of weeks, faster than the crowd can react. The stock then **holds almost all of that gain** in a tight sideways flag: weekly closes cluster near the prior week's close, the high-to-low spread of each week shrinks, and **volume dries up dramatically**. That dry-up is the tell — it means holders are *not* selling into the huge gain (little supply) while slow accumulation continues quietly, "not obvious to everyone." Little volume + a slight drift up = few people notice. When the flag breaks out on volume, the move extends — and because the base is so tight, the stop is close and the reward/risk is extreme. The rarity + the compact pole + the volume dry-up are what separate the real thing from a stock that's simply extended.

## Hunt / scan steps

1. **Screen for the compact pole first.** Find stocks up ~**100%+ in the last 4–8 weeks** — the shorter and steeper, the better. This is the gate; do it before you look at anything else. (A move that took 6 months disqualifies here, no matter how big.)
2. **Confirm the catalyst.** Is there a real new-product / new-issue re-rating and accelerating earnings? A little-known name, not the sector's largest, is the classic profile. No catalyst → downgrade to "extended stock," not HTF.
3. **Demand the tight flag.** 3–5 weeks (occasionally up to ~11 in a slower chain) of sideways action correcting only **10–25%**, with **each week closing tight to the prior week** and **shrinking high-low spreads**. If it corrects 30–40% or the ranges are wide/erratic, it's a different (looser) base.
4. **Require the volume dry-up.** Volume should contract *dramatically* through the flag — the "extreme volume dry-up" O'Neil marks by hand. High or erratic volume in the flag is churn/distribution, not the quiet accumulation this pattern needs.
5. **Buy the flag breakout** on a volume expansion above the flag's high, holding the rising 10-week. **Add on the first pullback to the 10-week MA.** Stop just below the flag (the tightness is the edge — the stop is close). See your position-sizing rules.
6. **Manage toward the climax top.** These run fast and end in a blow-off; O'Neil's own sell mark on every example is "climax top." Trail beneath the 10-week / swing lows and respect the parabolic exhaustion (cf. [[glossary#climax-top]]).

## Annotation checklist (the marks to draw, in order)

Draw these, left to right — they tell the story:

1. **The pole** — bracket the compact 100%+ advance and label its duration in weeks (this is the criterion most charts fail; make it explicit).
2. **The flag** — box the tight 3–5 week sideways range; note the % correction (should be 10–25%) and that weekly closes cluster tight.
3. **Volume dry-up** — mark the contracting volume through the flag (the accumulation footprint). Label "volume dry-up."
4. **The buy** — the breakout above the flag high on a volume expansion, holding the rising 10-week.
5. **Add / manage** — the first pullback to the 10-week (the add), and the eventual "sell: climax top" if the chart is a completed case study.

Keep it sparse. O'Neil's own charts carry ~4–6 marks. The pole-duration label is non-negotiable — it's the mark that proves it's an HTF at all.

## Eval rubric — a lens, not a checklist

> **Read this as a judge's bench notes, not a scorecard.** These are the criteria to *reason against* so grading is grounded and consistent — NOT boxes to tick. Judge the chart: note where a criterion is *partially* met, where a "failure" is actually instructive, and where the chart does something the criteria don't anticipate. **A chart can be excellent while missing a criterion, and weak while checking every box.** Anchors (all O'Neil case studies in the corpus):
> - *Textbook:* `1986-09-01-ADBE-W-high-tight-flag-oneil` — a 4-week flag right after IPO, extreme volume dry-up, closing tight to prior weeks with tiny spreads, strong fundamentals. O'Neil's cleanest narration of *why it worked*.
> - *The rarest / repeating:* `2003-12-01-TASR-W-high-tight-flag-oneil` — three HTFs off a big cup-with-handle base (+2228%/39wk). Shows the pattern *chains* and *repeats* on a true leader.
> - *Fastest / catalyst-driven:* `1963-06-01-SYNTEX-W-high-tight-flag-oneil` — "The Pill," huge earnings growth, +451%/25wk. The catalyst *is* the setup.
> - *Not-an-HTF (the discipline anchor):* `2026-06-22-ICHR-W-range-breakout-shankar-nath` — an explosive, real move, but the advance was a **multi-month Stage-2 trend, not a 4–8 week pole**. Correctly graded `range-breakout`, not HTF. Marking that absence is the *correct*, advanced read.

### Grade adversarially-first (the honesty protocol)

**The default failure mode is grading toward a grade — hunting reasons the setup WORKS and confirming the strong criteria.** That flatters every chart, especially one whose trend already ran (hindsight makes soft evidence look strong). And for the HTF specifically, the strongest pull is to *call something an HTF that isn't* — because the real thing is so lucrative, everything strong looks like one. Grade the other direction:

1. **Start by trying to DISQUALIFY it — and the first disqualifier is the pole.** Assume it's *not* an HTF and check the compact pole *first*: was the 100%+ move actually 4–8 weeks, or did it take months? If the pole is a multi-month grind, stop — it's a Stage-2 base / [[buying-in-bases]], not an HTF, and no amount of tightness in the flag rescues the label. Then hunt the other failure modes (flag too deep/loose >25%, volume *not* drying up, no real catalyst, it's the biggest name in the group, extended into the breakout with a far stop, post-climax rather than mid-trend).
2. **Give every criterion an explicit verdict: `pass` / `PARTIAL` / `fail`** — never a vague "present." A criterion is `PARTIAL` unless it clearly clears the strong-form bar (a pole that's 100% over ~10 weeks is `PARTIAL` on the compact-pole test, not a `pass`; a flag correcting ~28% is `PARTIAL`/`fail` on tightness; merely-below-average volume is `PARTIAL`, not the "extreme dry-up" `pass`). When unsure between two levels, take the *lower*.
3. **Name the single weakest piece and the one determining read** — for the HTF, that determining read is almost always *the pole's compactness* and *the volume dry-up*. If the honest tally is "one pass, two partials," say so plainly; don't average it up.
4. **No overall letter/level until every criterion has its verdict.** The grade is the *sum of the honest per-criterion reads*, not a gestalt impression. A chart with a stretched pole is not an A-HTF even if it later ran 300%.
5. **Ignore the outcome when grading the SETUP.** "It went up 400%" grades the market's verdict, not your read at the pivot — and it does NOT retroactively make a multi-month pole "compact." Grade the evidence visible *before* the breakout; let the outcome inform the *lesson*, not the grade.

The point is to learn to read the *proper* HTF — a genuinely rare thing — AND to honestly recognize how often a strong-looking chart is something else. A move honestly graded "not an HTF, it's a Stage-2 base" is worth more than a generous HTF label on the wrong structure. Being generous is the enemy of learning here.

Criteria to weigh:

1. **The compact pole (the gate, highest-value check).** 100–120%+ in **4–8 weeks** — steep and short. This is the single most-discriminating, most-skipped criterion. A big move that took months is *not* a pole; it fails here even if everything else is pretty. Judge duration explicitly, in weeks.
2. **Flag tightness — tight AND shallow.** 3–5 weeks sideways, correcting only **10–25%**, weekly closes clustered near the prior week's close, shrinking high-low spreads. A flag that corrects 30%+ or has wide/erratic weekly ranges is a looser base, not an HTF.
3. **Extreme volume dry-up.** Volume should contract *dramatically* through the flag — quiet, slow accumulation with little selling into the gain. High/erratic flag volume is churn or distribution (the same-size-bar-opposite-meaning read: mark where the flag's volume *sits*, not just that bars exist). No dry-up → not the pattern.
4. **Real catalyst + fundamentals.** A genuine new product / new-issue re-rating on a little-known company (not the group's largest) with accelerating earnings. The pattern without the story is the weak version O'Neil warns about — note its absence.
5. **Is the entry defensible?** Buy the flag breakout on volume, add on the first 10-week pullback, stop just below the tight flag. Buying extended *above* the flag with a far stop fails this even if 1–4 are "there." Is this mid-trend, or is it actually a post-[[glossary#climax-top|climax]] consolidation (a knife)?

When you grade: confirm the pole is compact, the flag is tight-and-light with a volume dry-up, and there's a real catalyst — then name the *one thing* that most determines whether it's a true HTF or a mislabeled base. Frame feedback in O'Neil's vocabulary.

## Common failure modes (what disqualifies or weakens it)

- **The pole took months (the #1 mis-call).** A 100%+ move over a multi-month Stage-2 advance is not a high tight flag — it's a base within a trend. This is the failure that most often earns a false HTF label (cf. the ICHR anchor).
- **Flag too deep / too loose** — correcting >25%, or wide erratic weekly ranges. That's a normal base, not the tight flag; the tightness is the whole edge.
- **No volume dry-up** — flat or erratic (or rising) volume through the flag signals churn/distribution, not the quiet accumulation the pattern needs.
- **No real catalyst / the biggest name in the group** — the well-known megacap "high tight flag" is usually the weak, over-called version; O'Neil's are little-known new-product movers.
- **Extended into the breakout** — buying well above the flag with a stop miles below forfeits the close-stop edge; wait for the flag pivot or the first 10-week pullback.
- **Post-climax, not mid-trend** — a tight range *after* a parabolic blow-off is exhaustion digesting, not an HTF; the next move down is a knife.
