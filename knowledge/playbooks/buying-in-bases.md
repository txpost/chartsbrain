# Playbook — Buying in Bases (Stage-2 continuation)

A **playbook** is a repeatable *procedure* plus an *eval rubric* — distinct from the [[glossary]] (definitions) and from a chart entry (one marked-up instance). This one covers **buying in bases**: entering *during* a controlled pullback/base *inside an already-established Stage-2 uptrend* — the continuation partner to the [[character-change]] playbook (which catches the trend's *birth*). Read on the **weekly**, with 10-week + 30-week SMAs + volume. Stage-Analysis-native (Nick Schmidt's framing of the Weinstein Stage-2 continuation).

Dual-purpose by design:
- **Prospective (hunting):** walk the steps to *find* and *mark up* a base candidate.
- **Retrospective (eval):** read the same criteria in reverse to *grade* a marked-up chart.

## When to use it

- A stock **already in a proven Stage-2 uptrend** (it had its character change; the MAs are stacked and rising) that is now **digesting** in a controlled pullback or base.
- **Weekly chart.** 10-week + 30-week SMAs + volume.
- The **first base after a [[character-change]]** is the highest-conviction version (no pyramiding risk — you're at the trend's start). In a sustained Stage-2 the pattern *repeats*, so you can also take the *next* base if you missed the first.
- *Not* for a stock still in a downtrend / just turning — that's the [[character-change]] playbook.

## The thesis

"Breakouts do not come from nowhere. They come from bases." A stock that already proved itself (trend up, ran hard) now digests. Institutions accumulate quietly during the base — filling a large order in a narrow range without pushing price out. Their footprint is the base coming up its right side **tighter and lighter**: higher lows, contracting candles, a volume dry-up. You enter *on weakness inside the base* (not chasing the breakout at the top of the range), which lets the stop sit under the most recent higher low at tight risk (3–5%) — bigger size, quieter timing, and 8–10% of cushion already built in by the time the breakout comes.

## Hunt / scan steps

1. **Start from proven Stage-2 leaders** — stacked, rising MAs; strong relative strength. (Best source: a name that just printed a [[character-change]] — watch for its *first* base.)
2. **Wait for the pullback to a rising MA.** Small runs pull back to the 10-week; bigger runs pull deeper to the 30-week. A deep pullback in a strong trend is the *bigger version* of the same pattern, not a trend break.
3. **Demand tightening + drying volume.** The base should get *tighter and lighter* up its right side: higher lows, contracting ranges, volume dry-up. This is the accumulation tell.
4. **Prize `ma-compression`** — the 10-week and 30-week converge tightly (within ~5%) while price coils and volume dries. Short- and intermediate-term holders share one cost basis; when compression + tightening + dry-up coincide, conviction is unusually high (cf. the ONDS example).
5. **Enter on weakness near a level** — a higher low, a weekly low, or a base-pivot retest — *not* strength at the top of the range. Stop under the most recent higher low (3–5%). See your position-sizing rules.
6. **Add only on fresh proof** — a new higher low, a push, or a breakout/retest. Don't pyramid into a mature, extended trend.

## Annotation checklist (the marks to draw)

1. **The prior uptrend / character change** — establish this is an *existing* Stage-2 (the MAs stacked and rising into the base).
2. **The base** — box it. Show the higher lows / tightening up the right side.
3. **Volume dry-up** — mark the contracting volume through the base (the quiet-accumulation footprint).
4. **The MA the base is built on** — the pullback holding the rising 10-week or 30-week (note `ma-compression` if the two MAs are converging).
5. **The entry** — on weakness near the level (higher low / pivot retest), with the tight stop shown just below.

## Eval rubric — a lens, not a checklist

> **Judge's bench notes, not a scorecard.** Reason against these criteria; note partial fits, instructive exceptions, and what the criteria don't cover. **A base can be excellent while imperfect (name the imperfection), and a clean-looking base can be a stay-away.** Anchors:
> - *Imperfect-but-tradeable:* a `buying-in-bases-imperfect` — the prior move was parabolic, or the base is long/loose — tradeable but lower-conviction; the annotation should *name what's not textbook*.
> - *Clean-looking-but-stay-away:* a tidy base on a **relatively-weak** name (down far more than the market) is still a stay-away — the pattern is fine, the context isn't.

### Grade adversarially-first (the honesty protocol)

**The default failure mode is grading toward a grade — hunting reasons the base WORKS and confirming the strong criteria.** That flatters every chart, especially one whose breakout already ran. Grade the other direction:

1. **Start by trying to DISQUALIFY it** — hunt the failure modes first (base loosening not tightening, volume *not* drying up / erratic, entry at the range top not on weakness, stop miles away, relative weakness, trend not actually established). Credit the strengths only after the bear case is exhausted.
2. **Give every criterion an explicit verdict: `pass` / `PARTIAL` / `fail`** — never a vague "present." Unless a criterion clearly clears the strong-form bar it's `PARTIAL` (a base tightening on *okay* volume is `PARTIAL`, not a `pass`). When unsure between two levels, take the *lower*.
3. **Name the single weakest piece and the one determining read.** If the honest tally is mostly partials, say so; don't average it up.
4. **No overall conviction level until every criterion has its verdict** — the grade is the sum of the honest per-criterion reads, not a gestalt.
5. **Ignore the outcome when grading the SETUP.** "The breakout ran" grades the market's verdict, not your read at the entry. An imperfect base that worked is still imperfect (tag `buying-in-bases-imperfect`) — grade the pre-breakout evidence, let the outcome inform the *lesson*.

The point is to learn to read *proper* setups AND to recognize honestly that they're often not perfect. Being generous is the enemy of learning here.

Criteria to weigh:

1. **Is the trend genuinely established?** This is a *continuation* setup — the stock must already be in a proven Stage-2 (character change done, MAs stacked and rising). A "base" on a stock that hasn't turned is a [[character-change]] question, not this.
2. **Is the base tightening — tighter AND lighter?** Higher lows + contracting ranges + volume dry-up, all three. A base on *high/erratic* volume is suspect (two-sided churn / distribution, not accumulation). The dry-up is the tell.
3. **Where's the entry — weakness or strength?** The edge is buying *on weakness near a level*, not chasing the breakout at the range top. An entry at the top of the range fails the playbook even if the base is pretty (this is the discipline that avoids the extended-into-pivot trap).
4. **Is the stop tight and structural?** Under the most recent higher low, ~3–5%. If the only sensible stop is far away, the base-buy window has likely closed (wait for the next base).
5. **`ma-compression` bonus.** If the 10-week and 30-week are converging tightly under coiling price with dry volume, that's the high-conviction signal — weight it up.
6. **Context / group.** Relative strength vs. the market, and whether the *group* is also setting up. A base matters more when money is moving in the cohort; a relatively-weak name with a clean base is still a stay-away.

When you grade: is the trend real, is the base tightening, and is the entry on weakness with a tight stop? Name the one thing that most determines conviction. Frame in Schmidt's + Weinstein's vocabulary.

## Common failure modes

- **Chasing the breakout at the top of the range** — the opposite of the playbook; wide stop, crowded timing.
- **Base on high/erratic volume** — churn or distribution, not accumulation; its breakout is less reliable.
- **Pyramiding into a mature, extended trend** — a base far up an old run has no cushion; each base must reset its own stop.
- **Post-climax consolidation** — a base after a *parabolic* blow-off behaves differently (longer, deeper, weaker tightening); `ma-compression` becomes the single strongest signal when other criteria are weak.
- **Relatively-weak name** — a clean base on a stock underperforming the market badly is a stay-away regardless of the pattern.
